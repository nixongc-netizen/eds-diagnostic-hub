The problem

Standing up shouldn't feel like a medical event.

You stand up from the couch and the room tilts. Your heart pounds like you just sprinted. Your vision narrows. Your brain goes offline. You sit back down and pretend nothing happened — because the last doctor told you it was anxiety.

This isn't anxiety. This is Gravity Sickness — the experience of a nervous system that can't regulate blood flow against gravity because the connective tissue holding your blood vessels open is structurally different.

The stand-up crash Dizziness, vision graying, or near-fainting within seconds of standing
The racing heart Heart rate spikes 30+ beats per minute just from being upright
The afternoon collapse Crushing fatigue and brain fog that peaks after hours of being vertical
The shower dread Hot showers trigger lightheadedness, nausea, or the feeling you might pass out
The grocery store wall Standing in line or walking through stores becomes impossible without sitting down
The invisible disability You look fine. You're not fine. Nobody understands why you can't just "push through it."
The cost of being dismissed

Every day without a plan is a day your world gets smaller.

POTS — Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome — affects an estimated 1–3 million Americans. In people with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome, the rate is closer to 50%. And yet the average POTS patient sees 7 doctors over 4 years before receiving a diagnosis.

Your blood vessels are made of connective tissue. In hEDS, that tissue is lax. When you stand, blood pools in your legs instead of reaching your brain. Your heart rate spikes to compensate. This isn't a mystery — it's physics.

While you wait for a diagnosis, your world contracts. You stop cooking. You stop shopping. You stop exercising. You stop leaving the house on bad days. The longer this goes unnamed and unmanaged, the more your autonomic nervous system deconditions — and the harder it gets to recover.

You don't need another doctor to tell you it's anxiety. You need a protocol written by a physician who understands the mechanics of why your body fights gravity — and exactly what to do about it.
