Genetic Testing & hEDS — What You Need to Know

Why there is no test for hEDS — and what testing IS worth doing

The Most Important Thing to Understand

There is currently no genetic test that can diagnose hEDS. This is not a gap in testing technology — it is a reflection of the current state of the science. The gene or genes responsible for hEDS have not yet been identified. The landmark HEDGE Study, which sequenced DNA from over 1,000 hEDS patients, confirmed in 2024 that no single gene explains the condition. hEDS remains a clinical diagnosis based on the 2017 international criteria — exactly what the MyEDSTest assessment is designed to screen for.

If a lab or provider is selling you a test that claims to "diagnose hEDS genetically," that claim is not currently supported by science. hEDS cannot be diagnosed by a blood test or saliva kit as of 2026.

Why Genetic Testing Still Matters — Ruling Out Dangerous Alternatives

Even though hEDS has no genetic test, genetic testing is still critically important for people with suspected hEDS. The reason: there are other connective tissue disorders that look like hEDS but have identified genetic mutations — and some of them are life-threatening. Testing is how you rule them out.

Vascular EDS (vEDS) — COL3A1 gene

The most dangerous EDS subtype. Characterized by spontaneous rupture of arteries and hollow organs. Can cause sudden death. Many vEDS patients have relatively mild skin and joint findings — making it easy to miss. If you have any family history of unexplained early death, aortic dissection, or arterial rupture, genetic testing to rule out vEDS is essential.

Marfan Syndrome — FBN1 gene

Affects the aorta, eyes, and skeleton. Associated with aortic aneurysm. Often presents with hypermobility, tall stature, and long limbs. Requires cardiac surveillance if confirmed. Treatable with medication to slow aortic dilation.

Loeys-Dietz Syndrome — TGFBR1, TGFBR2, others

An aggressive aortic disorder that can look like Marfan syndrome. High risk of aortic dissection at smaller aortic sizes than Marfan syndrome. May present with joint hypermobility, easy bruising, and bifid uvula. Requires urgent cardiac management if diagnosed.

Classical EDS (cEDS) — COL5A1, COL5A2 genes

Presents with significant skin fragility, wide atrophic scars, and joint hypermobility. More severe than hEDS in terms of tissue fragility. Genetic confirmation allows for appropriate surgical precautions and family counseling.

Who Should Consider Genetic Testing

  • Anyone with a family history of early unexplained death, aortic dissection, or sudden arterial rupture — rule out vEDS urgently
  • Anyone with tall stature, unusually long limbs, lens displacement, or aortic dilation on imaging — rule out Marfan syndrome
  • Anyone with significant skin fragility, wide scars, or arterial aneurysms — rule out classical or vascular EDS
  • Anyone whose physician wants to confirm the diagnosis before major surgery
  • Anyone planning a family who wants genetic counseling on recurrence risk
  • Anyone whose symptoms are atypical or who doesn't fully meet hEDS criteria — a panel helps identify less common connective tissue disorders
Most straightforward hEDS presentations do not require genetic testing. Genetic testing is most valuable when there is diagnostic uncertainty or when dangerous alternative diagnoses need to be excluded.

If your genetic panel comes back negative — no pathogenic variants found — this does not mean you don't have a connective tissue disorder. It means you don't have one of the known genetic forms. A negative panel in someone who clinically meets hEDS criteria is expected and normal. It supports the diagnosis of hEDS (which requires ruling out other genetic CTDs) and rules out the more dangerous subtypes. This is a good outcome.

Section 1: POTS & Dysautonomia

Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome

What is POTS?

POTS is very common in hEDS. When you stand up, your body normally tightens blood vessels quickly so blood doesn't pool in your legs. In hEDS, connective tissue is looser — including the walls of blood vessels — so blood pools when you stand. Your heart speeds up to try to compensate. This causes dizziness, brain fog, racing heart, and fatigue that gets worse when upright.

Home Testing with a Heart Rate Monitor

You can do a simple home screening test called the NASA Lean Test using a smartwatch, Fitbit, or any pulse oximeter:

Step-by-step

  1. Lie down flat for 10 minutes. Record your heart rate.
  2. Stand up slowly and stay still (lean against a wall if needed).
  3. Record your heart rate at 1, 3, 5, and 10 minutes of standing.
  4. A heart rate increase of 30+ beats per minute (or 40+ if under 19) is a positive screen.
  5. Note symptoms: dizziness, brain fog, heart pounding, feeling faint.
Important: A home test is a screen, not a diagnosis. A positive result means you should see a cardiologist or autonomic neurologist for a formal tilt-table test.

Getting a Formal Diagnosis

Ask your primary care doctor for a referral to cardiology or neurology for a tilt-table test — the gold standard for diagnosing POTS. When you call, mention you have hEDS or suspected hEDS and want to be evaluated for dysautonomia/POTS.

General POTS Management Strategies

General strategies discussed in the medical literature. Not a substitute for care with a cardiologist. Discuss with your doctor before starting.

Salt & Fluid Loading

Many POTS patients benefit from 2–3 liters of water and 3,000–5,000mg sodium daily. Salt tablets (OTC) make this easier. Check with your doctor first — not appropriate for everyone.

Compression Garments

20–30 mmHg compression stockings reduce blood pooling. Waist-high is more effective than knee-high. Put them on before getting out of bed.

Exercise — Recumbent Approach

Start with rowing machines, recumbent bikes, or swimming. Gradually build upright tolerance over months. One of the best long-term treatments.

Lifestyle Adjustments

  • Elevate head of bed 4–6 inches
  • Avoid hot showers (worsen pooling)
  • Eat small, frequent meals
  • Sit at bed edge 1–2 min before standing
  • Avoid prolonged standing — shift weight

Stellate Ganglion Block for POTS and Anxiety

The stellate ganglion is a bundle of nerves in your neck that controls part of the sympathetic ("fight-or-flight") nervous system. In POTS, the sympathetic nervous system is often overactive. A stellate ganglion block (SGB) is an injection performed by an interventional pain physician that temporarily quiets this overactive system.

Research — including military studies on PTSD — shows SGB can significantly reduce sympathetic hyperactivity. In POTS and hEDS patients, it may help with racing heart, anxiety, and the constant feeling of being "on edge." Results typically last several months to over a year.

Who to ask

An interventional pain physician or anesthesiologist who performs fluoroscopy-guided or ultrasound-guided neck injections. Mention you are interested in SGB for dysautonomia/POTS.

Section 2: Mast Cell & Histamine (MCAS)

Mast Cell Activation Syndrome

What is MCAS?

Mast cells are immune cells found throughout your body. In MCAS, they release histamine and other chemicals too easily — causing flushing, hives, gut problems, brain fog, and severe reactions to foods, smells, or medications. MCAS is very common in hEDS because mast cells are embedded in connective tissue.

The Low-Histamine Diet

Histamine is found in high amounts in aged, fermented, and processed foods. Reducing these foods can significantly reduce MCAS symptoms.

Avoid or limit

  • Aged cheeses
  • Fermented foods (kombucha, sauerkraut)
  • Wine, beer, champagne
  • Smoked or cured meats
  • Canned fish (tuna, sardines)
  • Tomatoes, spinach, avocado
  • Vinegar and pickled foods
  • Leftovers (histamine builds with time)

Generally well tolerated

  • Fresh meat and fish (cooked same day)
  • Most fresh vegetables
  • Rice, quinoa, millet
  • Fresh eggs
  • Coconut oil, olive oil
  • Fresh herbs
  • Most fruit (except citrus, strawberries)

Tip: Fresh is best. Histamine rises as food ages. Cook fresh and eat promptly.

OTC Trial: H1 + H2 Blocker Protocol

A simple first step is a trial of over-the-counter antihistamines targeting both H1 and H2 receptors. Significant symptom improvement supports a histamine/mast cell component to your condition.

H1 Blocker — Levocetirizine (Xyzal) 5mg once daily

Or Cetirizine (Zyrtec) 10mg once daily. Xyzal is the more potent version. Take at bedtime. Blocks H1 histamine receptors responsible for flushing, itching, and allergy-type reactions.

H2 Blocker — Famotidine (Pepcid) 20mg twice daily

Blocks H2 receptors in the gut and on mast cells. Combined with H1 blocker, this dual approach is standard in MCAS management. Take with food.

Trial for 4–6 weeks. If significant symptom relief occurs, discuss with your doctor about formal MCAS workup (serum tryptase, 24-hour urine methylhistamine). Check for drug interactions if taking other medications.

Section 3: Supplements & Anti-Inflammatory Support

7 evidence-backed options for inflammation and recovery

1

Quercetin

A natural flavonoid and mast cell stabilizer — helps prevent mast cells from releasing histamine. Also has antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. Especially useful if MCAS is a component.

Typical dose: 500–1,000mg twice daily with food. Use quercetin phytosome or combine with bromelain for better absorption.

2

Turmeric (Curcumin)

Curcumin blocks several inflammatory pathways (including NF-kB) that drive chronic pain and inflammation. Well-studied in joint pain and inflammatory conditions.

Typical dose: 500–1,500mg curcumin extract daily. Must be taken with black pepper (piperine) or in liposomal form to absorb properly. Standard turmeric powder absorbs poorly.

3

Fish Oil (Omega-3 Fatty Acids)

EPA and DHA reduce inflammatory signaling throughout the body. Especially useful for joint pain, brain fog, and cardiovascular health. One of the best-studied supplements in existence.

Typical dose: 2–4g combined EPA+DHA daily. Choose triglyceride-form fish oil from a reputable brand that tests for heavy metals. Take with a fatty meal.

4

Cold-Pressed Cacao

Raw cacao is rich in flavonoids (especially epicatechin) that support blood vessel health, reduce inflammation, and have mood-enhancing properties. Also contains magnesium — deficient in most hEDS patients. Avoid if histamine-sensitive.

How to use: 1–2 tablespoons of cold-pressed cacao powder in smoothies or warm drinks daily. Look for ceremonial grade or cold-pressed raw cacao.

5

PEA (Palmitoylethanolamide)

A naturally occurring fatty acid that works on mast cells and nerve cells to reduce chronic pain and inflammation. Particularly useful for nerve pain, fibromyalgia-type symptoms, and widespread hEDS pain. Well-tolerated and non-psychoactive.

Typical dose: 600–1,200mg daily. Use micronized or ultramicronized form (PEA-m or PEA-um). May take 4–8 weeks to see full benefit.

6

Magnesium Glycinate

Magnesium deficiency worsens muscle pain, headaches, anxiety, and poor sleep — all common in hEDS. Magnesium glycinate is the most absorbable and gentlest form on the gut. Also supports sleep when taken at night.

Typical dose: 200–400mg elemental magnesium at bedtime. Avoid magnesium oxide — poorly absorbed and causes diarrhea.

7

Vitamin D3 + K2

Critical for immune function, muscle strength, pain modulation, and mood. Deficiency is almost universal in Alaska and in patients with chronic fatigue. K2 (MK-7 form) directs calcium to bones rather than arteries.

Typical dose: 2,000–5,000 IU D3 daily with 100mcg K2 (MK-7). Check your blood level (25-OH Vitamin D) before dosing — aim for 50–80 ng/mL.

Section 4: Pain by Body Region

Common hEDS findings and where to start

In hEDS, pain has patterns. The same structures fail in predictable ways. Understanding what's likely causing your pain helps you ask for the right workup. All of these require evaluation by a physician for proper diagnosis and treatment planning.

Neck Pain

Cervicogenic Headaches

Pain that starts in the neck and radiates to the back of the head, temples, or behind the eyes. Often worse with screen use, prolonged sitting, or wrong sleep position. Caused by instability in the upper cervical (C1–C3) joints.

Where to start: PT focused on cervical stabilization (not stretching). Avoid forceful neck manipulation. Ask your pain physician about cervical facet injections if PT doesn't help.

Occipital Neuralgia

Sharp, shooting, or electric pain at the base of the skull radiating up and over the head. Caused by irritation of the occipital nerves where they pass through tight, lax neck muscles and connective tissue. Very treatable.

Where to start: Ask your pain physician about greater occipital nerve blocks. Many patients get significant relief from a simple injection. Radiofrequency ablation (RFA) can provide longer-lasting results.

Back Pain

Sacroiliac (SI) Joint Pain

The SI joints connect your spine to your pelvis. In hEDS, these joints are hypermobile and unstable, causing pain in the lower back, buttock, and upper leg. Often misdiagnosed as disc herniation. Worsens with prolonged sitting, standing on one leg, or rolling over in bed.

Where to start: SI joint stabilization exercises with a hypermobility-aware PT. SI joint injections for diagnosis and pain relief. Prolotherapy or PRP injections can help stabilize the joint. Ask an interventional pain physician.

Lumbar Facet Pain

Small joints on the back of the spine that bear excess load in hEDS. Deep, aching low back pain that is worse in the morning, after staying in one position, or with back extension (bending backward). Usually no leg pain.

Where to start: Ask your pain physician about medial branch nerve blocks for diagnosis. If positive, radiofrequency ablation (RFA) can provide 12–18 months of relief. Highly effective treatment for facet pain.

Disc Disease — More Common in hEDS Than Generally Recognized

Disc degeneration, bulges, and herniations occur at higher rates in hEDS. Lax spinal ligaments allow abnormal segmental motion that accelerates disc wear. Lumbar disc problems cause lower back pain that may radiate down the leg (sciatica). Cervical disc problems cause neck pain with arm pain or numbness. A significant disc problem will show on MRI — but many hEDS patients have findings that look alarming and are actually manageable conservatively. Do not rush to surgery.

Where to start: MRI of the affected spine segment. Epidural steroid injections are highly effective for disc-related nerve pain. Long-term: spinal stabilization PT and core strengthening. Disc surgery in hEDS has a higher complication and failure rate than in non-hypermobile patients — exhaust all conservative options first, and ensure your surgeon is aware of your connective tissue diagnosis.

Shoulder Pain

Shoulder Instability & Rotator Cuff Strain

Loose ligaments allow the shoulder ball to shift slightly, straining the rotator cuff muscles that work overtime to stabilize it. Clicking, pain reaching overhead, or a sense the shoulder "slips." Dislocations and subluxations are common.

Where to start: PT focused on rotator cuff and scapular strengthening — not stretching. Bracing during high-demand activities. PRP injections to stabilize tendons and ligaments through an orthobiologic physician.

Rib Pain & Rib Subluxations

Costochondral & Costovertebral Instability

Rib pain is one of the most overlooked and misdiagnosed symptoms in hEDS. Ribs attach to the spine in the back (costovertebral joints) and to the sternum or cartilage in the front (costochondral joints). In hEDS, the ligaments holding these joints in place are lax, allowing ribs to shift — called a rib subluxation. This causes sharp, catching pain with deep breathing, rolling over, sneezing, coughing, reaching overhead, or sleeping on one side. It is often mistaken for heart or lung problems, pleurisy, or intercostal neuralgia.

Patients often describe feeling or hearing a "pop" followed by intense local pain that resolves when the rib moves back. The pain is usually very localized — you can point to exactly where it hurts — and worsens with pressure on the affected area.

Common areas: Along the side of the ribcage (floating ribs 11–12 most mobile), under the breast near the sternum (costochondritis), and between the shoulder blades where ribs meet the thoracic spine.

When to Seek Urgent Evaluation

Rib pain in hEDS is usually musculoskeletal. However, always rule out cardiac, pulmonary, or aortic causes with your physician first — especially if the pain is new, severe, accompanied by shortness of breath, radiates down the arm, or is associated with swelling or bruising without injury.

Once cardiac/pulmonary causes are excluded: A plain chest X-ray and/or CT can confirm rib position. Your pain physician can perform diagnostic intercostal nerve blocks or costovertebral joint injections to confirm the source.

Referral to an hEDS-Friendly Chiropractor

Chiropractic care in hEDS must be approached carefully. High-velocity, forceful manipulation (the classic "crack") is generally not appropriate for hypermobile patients — it can worsen instability. However, chiropractors who specialize in low-force, instrument-assisted, or soft-tissue techniques can be very helpful for rib subluxations specifically, because gently repositioning a subluxed rib is one of the most effective treatments available.

What to look for in a chiropractor:
  • Experience with EDS, hypermobility, or connective tissue disorders
  • Uses low-force or instrument-based techniques (Activator method, Torque Release, SOT)
  • Does NOT perform forceful cervical (neck) manipulation on hEDS patients
  • Works collaboratively with your pain physician and PT team
How to find one: Search the EDS Society provider directory at ehlers-danlos.com/healthcare-professionals-directory and filter for chiropractic. You can also ask your pain physician or PT for a trusted referral — providers who already work with hEDS patients often have colleagues they recommend. When calling, ask directly: "Do you have experience treating patients with hypermobile EDS? Do you use low-force techniques?"

Self-Management Between Appointments

  • Avoid sleeping on the affected side — a body pillow supporting the ribcage helps
  • Kinesiology tape applied along the affected rib can provide support and pain relief
  • Avoid heavy lifting, overhead reaching, or twisting movements during a flare
  • Heat applied to the area can ease muscle spasm around the subluxed rib
  • Diaphragmatic (belly) breathing rather than chest breathing reduces mechanical stress on the ribs during recovery

Hip Pain

Hip Labral Tears & Snapping Hip

Hypermobility causes repetitive micro-trauma that tears the hip labrum over time. Groin pain, a clicking or snapping sensation, and pain with prolonged sitting or activity. "Snapping hip" (tendons flicking over bony prominences) is also common.

Where to start: Hip stabilization PT (strengthening glutes, hip external rotators). Hip joint injections for diagnosis and pain relief. PRP can help with labral healing. Avoid high-impact activity until stabilized.

Knee Pain

Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome

The kneecap tracks off-center due to weak and lax supporting structures, causing pain around or behind the kneecap with stairs, squatting, sitting for long periods, or running. Knees may also hyperextend (bend backward), causing chronic strain.

Where to start: PT focused on VMO (inner quad) and hip strengthening, patellar bracing or taping. Avoid deep squats and lunges initially. PRP for chronic knee pain and ligament laxity through an orthobiologic physician. Wear supportive footwear.

Hand & Finger Pain

Small Joint Hypermobility & Instability

Finger and thumb joints that hyperextend, causing pain with gripping, typing, writing, or carrying. Fingers may swell, ache, or feel clumsy. Thumbs are especially vulnerable at the CMC joint at the base.

Where to start: Occupational therapy (OT) for hand exercises and splinting. Silver ring splints are the gold standard for finger hypermobility — they allow movement while preventing hyperextension. Search "Oval-8 splints" or "Silver Ring Splint Company." Avoid tight gripping during flares.

Hand & Arm Numbness and Tingling

Numbness, tingling, or "pins and needles" in the hands and arms is very common in hEDS and is often attributed to a single cause when there are actually several distinct syndromes involved. Getting the right diagnosis determines the treatment.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (median nerve at the wrist)

The most common cause. The median nerve is compressed at the wrist. In hEDS, lax wrist ligaments combined with repetitive activity (typing, phone use) make this especially common. Classic pattern: numbness and tingling in the thumb, index, and middle fingers. Worse at night and with prolonged wrist flexion. Confirmed by nerve conduction study (NCS/EMG).

Treatment: Night splints, activity modification, corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel. Surgical release is highly effective if conservative measures fail — discuss hEDS healing considerations with your surgeon.

Cervicobrachial Syndrome (neck-to-arm nerve pain)

Numbness that originates in the cervical spine and radiates down the arm. Caused by nerve root compression from disc bulges, facet overgrowth, or cervical instability — all of which are more common in hEDS. Each nerve root has a predictable territory: C6 → thumb/index; C7 → middle finger; C8 → ring/little finger. Symptoms often also involve the arm and shoulder, and change with neck position — distinguishing it from carpal tunnel.

Treatment: Cervical spine MRI to identify the level. Cervical epidural steroid injections or selective nerve root blocks are very effective for relief. Cervical stabilization PT addresses the underlying instability long-term.

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (brachial plexus compression)

Less recognized but frequently missed. The brachial plexus is compressed between the collarbone and first rib. In hEDS, postural changes, drooping shoulders, and hypermobile ribs make this more common. Symptoms: diffuse arm heaviness, fatigue, and whole-hand or forearm numbness — worse with overhead activity or carrying loads.

Treatment: Provocative exam tests (Adson's, EAST test) can suggest diagnosis. PT focused on posture, rib mobility, and scalene muscle lengthening is first-line. Refer to vascular or thoracic specialist for confirmation.

Key distinction: Thumb/index/middle numbness worse at night → carpal tunnel. Numbness following a strip down the arm that changes with neck position → cervicobrachial. Whole arm heaviness with overhead use → thoracic outlet. A nerve conduction study (NCS/EMG) and cervical MRI can sort these out definitively — ask your pain physician or neurologist.

Section 5: Physical Therapy Resources

Finding the right PT for hypermobility

What to Look for in a PT

Not all physical therapists understand hypermobility. A PT who doesn't know hEDS may hurt you by pushing you to stretch more (you're already too flexible) or loading you too aggressively. You need a PT who understands stability over flexibility.

  • Ask: "Do you have experience treating hEDS or hypermobility spectrum disorder?"
  • Ask: "Do you focus on stability and proprioception rather than stretching?"
  • Red flag: A PT who gives aggressive stretching exercises
  • Red flag: A PT who loads you heavily right away without testing your baseline

The Muldowney Protocol

A structured, progressive exercise program developed specifically for hEDS and hypermobility spectrum disorder. Available as a book and through certified PTs. Search "Muldowney Protocol hEDS" — widely regarded as one of the best-structured approaches to hypermobility rehab.

Finding a Provider

Telehealth PT: Several hypermobility-specialist PTs offer online programs. Search "hypermobility PT telehealth." Jeannie Di Bon (Zebra Club) is commonly referenced in the hEDS community for online programs.

Section 6: Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN)

An emerging option for chronic pain and inflammation

What is LDN?

Naltrexone is FDA-approved at high doses (50mg) for opioid and alcohol use disorder. At very low doses (1.5–4.5mg), it has a completely different effect — it briefly blocks opioid receptors, which causes the body to increase its own endorphin production and reduce neuroinflammation. This is Low Dose Naltrexone, or LDN.

LDN has been studied in fibromyalgia, Crohn's disease, MS, and other chronic inflammatory conditions with promising results. Many hEDS patients and physicians report significant improvement in pain, fatigue, and brain fog.

Key points

  • Requires a prescription — must be compounded (standard tablets are 50mg)
  • Cannot be taken with opioid pain medications
  • Very safe side effect profile at low doses
  • Takes 4–12 weeks to see full benefit
  • Typically $30–60/month at compounding pharmacies

How to Find a Prescriber

Search ldnresearchtrust.org for a prescriber directory. You can also ask your primary care doctor or pain physician — LDN is increasingly prescribed for chronic pain. Telehealth physicians are often willing to prescribe for established chronic pain conditions.

Section 7: Sleep & Circadian Rhythm

The foundation of fatigue recovery

Morning Sunlight & POMC Stimulation

Morning sunlight activates POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin) — a master hormone precursor that breaks down into alpha-MSH, ACTH, and beta-endorphin, your body's natural pain-killing and mood-regulating molecules. This is one reason sunlight has such a profound effect on pain, mood, and energy.

Morning Light Protocol

  • Get outdoor sunlight within 30–60 minutes of waking
  • 10–20 minutes minimum — without sunglasses (glasses okay)
  • Cloudy days still work — outdoor light is 10–50x brighter than indoor light
  • In Alaska winters: use a 10,000 lux light therapy box for 20–30 minutes upon waking

Red & Near-Infrared (NIR) Light Therapy

Red light (630–670nm) and near-infrared (810–850nm) penetrate tissue and are absorbed by mitochondria, stimulating ATP production, reducing inflammation, and promoting tissue repair.

For pain & inflammation

Apply to painful joints for 10–15 minutes. Look for panels with both 660nm (red) and 850nm (NIR) wavelengths. Consistent use shows significant pain reduction in many patients.

For sleep — evening use

Red light does not suppress melatonin like blue light does. Use a red light panel for 10–15 minutes in the evening in place of bright overhead lights after sunset.

Device note: Joovv, Mito Red Light, and similar brands. Look for at least 50 mW/cm² irradiance at 6 inches. Avoid cheap panels that don't specify wavelength output.

5 Circadian Sleep Principles

1
Wake same time every day — even weekends. The single most important thing for circadian rhythm. Your wake time determines when melatonin rises at night.
2
No screens 1 hour before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin. Switch to red/amber lighting after sunset. Blue light blocking glasses help if screen use is unavoidable.
3
Cool bedroom (65–68°F) — sleep onset requires core body temperature to drop. A warm shower 1–2 hours before bed paradoxically helps — the subsequent cooling triggers sleepiness.
4
Low histamine before bed — for MCAS patients, nighttime histamine reactions can wake you from deep sleep. Eat your last meal 3 hours before bed. Your H1 antihistamine at night also helps.
5
Melatonin — low dose, timed right — 0.3–1mg taken 1–2 hours before target sleep time. High doses (5–10mg) don't work better and cause next-day grogginess. Timing matters more than dose.

Section 8: Peptides

BPC-157, Pinealon, and Epithalon
Research status: These peptides are research compounds studied in scientific literature. They are not FDA-approved medications and are not available at standard pharmacies. This information is educational. Discuss with a physician familiar with peptide therapy before considering any of these.

BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound)

BPC-157 is a synthetic peptide derived from a protein found in gastric juice. Extensively studied in animal models for accelerating healing of tendons, ligaments, muscles, and gut lining. Also appears to reduce pain sensitization and support dopamine and serotonin pathways.

Potential benefits studied

  • Tendon and ligament healing
  • Gut lining repair (leaky gut)
  • Joint pain reduction
  • Nerve pain modulation
  • Reduced systemic inflammation

Administration

  • Subcutaneous injection (most studied)
  • Oral capsules (gut effects)
  • Nasal spray
  • Typical dose: 250–500mcg daily

Of the three peptides listed here, BPC-157 is most relevant to hEDS due to its specific effects on connective tissue healing. Most human experience comes from clinical observation and case reports rather than large randomized trials.

Pinealon

A tripeptide (Glu-Asp-Arg) derived from the pineal gland. Research suggests it may support neuroregeneration, improve cognitive function, regulate sleep-wake cycles, and have antioxidant effects in brain tissue. Works partly by modulating circadian rhythm gene expression.

Most relevant for hEDS patients with:

  • Significant brain fog or cognitive symptoms
  • Disrupted sleep-wake cycles
  • Fatigue unresponsive to standard approaches

Typical use: 5–10mg in cycles of 10–20 days, as a nasal spray or sublingual drop. Most research is from Russian biogerontology literature — limited English-language RCT data.

Epithalon (Epitalon)

A tetrapeptide (Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly) developed by Professor Vladimir Khavinson. Research suggests it activates telomerase (which protects chromosome ends), regulates circadian rhythm through the melatonin pathway, and has antioxidant and immune-regulating effects.

Research suggests benefits in

  • Sleep normalization
  • Antioxidant protection
  • Immune regulation
  • Cellular longevity support

Typical use

  • 5–10mg per day
  • Cycles of 10–20 days
  • Subcutaneous or nasal
  • 1–2 cycles per year

Most useful for hEDS patients where sleep disruption and fatigue are primary concerns, under physician supervision as part of a broader recovery protocol.

Section 9: Pacing & Energy Management

Breaking the push-crash cycle

The Push-Crash Cycle

On good days, hEDS patients often overdo it — catching up on everything they couldn't do during the last flare. This leads to a crash: severe fatigue, increased pain, and reduced function that wipes out the progress made. The cycle repeats, often getting worse over time.

Pacing breaks this cycle by staying within your "energy envelope" — the amount of activity your body can handle without triggering a crash. It's not about doing less forever. It's about doing the right amount consistently so your capacity can gradually grow.

Heart Rate-Based Pacing

For POTS patients especially, heart rate is a direct measure of how hard your autonomic nervous system is working. Exceeding your aerobic threshold triggers sympathetic overdrive — fatigue, brain fog, and crashes. Heart rate monitoring gives you objective feedback in real time.

Find your aerobic threshold (rough estimate)

The most commonly used formula for POTS patients: Threshold ≈ 0.6 × (220 − your age)

Example: Age 35 → 220 − 35 = 185 → 0.6 × 185 = 111 bpm. Try to keep your heart rate below this during all activity.

Using it practically

  • Wear your smartwatch or heart rate monitor during all activity — including housework and cooking
  • When your HR approaches your threshold — slow down, sit, or rest immediately
  • Even activities that don't feel like "exercise" raise HR significantly in POTS
  • Gradually increase activity as your tolerance builds — add no more than 10% per week

The Energy Envelope

Think of your daily energy as a battery with a fixed capacity. The goal is to consistently operate at 70–80% — never fully depleting, but never fully resting either. This keeps the envelope stable and allows it to slowly grow over time.

What drains the battery fast

  • Upright activity (POTS)
  • Heat exposure
  • Emotional stress
  • Illness or infection
  • Poor sleep the night before
  • Skipping meals or dehydration
  • Sensory overload (crowds, noise)

What recharges and protects

  • Horizontal rest (lying down)
  • Cool environments
  • Consistent sleep schedule
  • Hydration and salt intake
  • Planned rest breaks during activity
  • Compression garments
  • Low-stimulation recovery time

Practical Pacing Strategies

Plan

Schedule rest before you need it. Build 10–15 minute horizontal rest breaks into your day proactively — not after a crash. Pre-activity rest is as important as post-activity rest.

Chunk

Break tasks into smaller pieces. Instead of cooking a full meal, prep for 10 minutes, sit for 5, cook for 10. The total activity is the same but the HR spike is smaller and recovery is faster.

Track

Keep a simple daily log. Rate your energy (1–10) morning and evening and note what you did. Patterns emerge over 2–4 weeks showing which activities most reliably drain you.

Communicate

Tell the people around you. Energy limitation is invisible. Explaining the push-crash cycle to family, friends, or employers prevents the misunderstanding that you're fine one day and "giving up" the next.

Section 10: Nutrition for Connective Tissue Repair

Eating to support collagen synthesis and reduce inflammation

Why Nutrition Matters in hEDS

In hEDS, the collagen your body produces is structurally abnormal — but the quality of collagen you make is still influenced by what you eat. Several nutrients are essential cofactors in collagen synthesis and cross-linking. Being deficient in any of them limits your body's ability to build the best connective tissue it can. Combined with an anti-inflammatory eating pattern, nutrition is one of the highest-leverage lifestyle changes in hEDS.

The Collagen Synthesis Nutrients

Vitamin C — the most critical collagen cofactor

Vitamin C is required for hydroxylation of proline and lysine — the step that stabilizes and cross-links collagen fibers. Without adequate Vitamin C, collagen cannot form properly. hEDS patients often need higher amounts due to ongoing tissue repair demands.

Food sources: Bell peppers (highest), kiwi, broccoli, guava. Avoid citrus and strawberries if histamine-sensitive. Supplement: 500–1,000mg buffered Vitamin C daily in divided doses.

Glycine & Proline — the structural amino acids of collagen

Collagen is made primarily of glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline. Getting adequate protein — especially from collagen-rich sources — provides the raw materials your body needs.

Food sources: Bone broth (highest in glycine and proline), skin-on poultry, fish with skin, egg whites, gelatin. Supplement: Hydrolyzed collagen peptides 10–15g daily combined with Vitamin C — the combination is synergistic.

Copper — the cross-linking enzyme activator

Copper activates lysyl oxidase, the enzyme that cross-links collagen and elastin fibers, giving them tensile strength. Copper deficiency directly weakens connective tissue and is frequently overlooked.

Food sources: Beef liver (richest source), oysters, dark chocolate, cashews, lentils. Note: If supplementing zinc, balance with 1–2mg copper for every 15–30mg zinc.

Zinc — tissue repair and wound healing

Zinc is required for over 300 enzymatic reactions including collagen synthesis and wound healing. Deficiency is common in hEDS and worsens tissue fragility and slow recovery from injury.

Food sources: Beef, oysters, pumpkin seeds, chickpeas. Supplement: 15–30mg zinc bisglycinate daily with food. Balance with copper (see above).

Anti-Inflammatory Eating Principles

Emphasize

  • Fatty fish 3x/week (salmon, mackerel)
  • Olive oil as primary cooking fat
  • Colorful vegetables — diverse daily
  • Berries (polyphenols and antioxidants)
  • Grass-fed beef and lamb
  • Bone broth regularly
  • Whole food carbs (rice, sweet potato, oats)

Minimize

  • Seed oils (canola, soybean, corn)
  • Ultra-processed foods
  • Added sugar (drives inflammation and destabilizes mast cells)
  • Alcohol (worsens POTS, MCAS, and gut permeability)
  • Gluten (trial elimination if gut symptoms prominent)
  • Dairy (trial elimination if MCAS suspected)

Gut Health & hEDS

Gut issues are extremely common in hEDS — the same lax connective tissue that affects joints also affects the gut lining, increasing intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), worsening MCAS, and driving IBS-type symptoms. Supporting gut integrity is high-leverage.

  • Bone broth — rich in glycine and gelatin, directly supports gut lining repair
  • L-Glutamine 5–10g daily — primary fuel for intestinal epithelial cells
  • Zinc carnosine 75mg daily — specifically studied for gut lining repair
  • Probiotics — choose low-histamine strains if MCAS is present. Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG and Bifidobacterium longum are generally better tolerated than broad-spectrum fermented products

Section 11: Flare Protocol

What to do when everything gets worse

What is a Flare?

A flare is a period of significantly worsened symptoms — increased pain, fatigue, joint instability, brain fog, MCAS reactions, or POTS symptoms — that goes beyond your usual baseline. Flares can last hours to weeks. Common triggers include illness, stress, hormonal changes, overexertion, weather changes, and disrupted sleep.

ER vs. Ride It Out

Go to the ER immediately for:

  • Chest pain with shortness of breath
  • Sudden severe tearing chest or back pain
  • Severe anaphylaxis — throat swelling, difficulty breathing, BP drop
  • Joint dislocation that won't reduce
  • Sudden severe headache ("worst of your life")
  • Fainting that doesn't resolve with lying flat
  • Sudden weakness, vision changes, or difficulty speaking

Manage at home, follow up with doctor:

  • Worsened widespread pain without new injury
  • Increased fatigue and brain fog
  • Flare of known POTS symptoms
  • MCAS reaction without throat involvement
  • Joint subluxation that reduced on its own
  • Increased sensory sensitivity or anxiety
If you're unsure whether a symptom is an emergency, call a nurse triage line. In hEDS, symptoms that resemble typical flares can occasionally have serious causes — don't dismiss severe or new symptoms.

The Flare Kit — Have These Ready

Medications & supplements

  • H1 antihistamine (Xyzal or Zyrtec)
  • H2 antihistamine (Pepcid 20mg)
  • Electrolyte sachets (for POTS flares)
  • Magnesium glycinate (muscle spasm and sleep)
  • Acetaminophen for pain — better tolerated in MCAS than NSAIDs
  • All regular medications — don't let them run out

Physical supports

  • Compression stockings — accessible, not buried in a drawer
  • Heating pad for muscle and joint pain
  • Ice packs for acute joint inflammation
  • Kinesiology tape for rib or joint support
  • Splints for frequently subluxing joints
  • Body pillow for sleep positioning

First 24 Hours of a Flare

1

Stop and rest immediately. Do not push through. The sooner you rest, the shorter the flare. Going horizontal takes load off your joints and reduces POTS-mediated stress on your system.

2

Hydrate with electrolytes. Dehydration worsens every hEDS symptom. Add salt and electrolytes — not just plain water.

3

Take your antihistamines if MCAS is involved. Don't wait to see if it passes. Early antihistamine use blunts the mast cell cascade before it escalates.

4

Eat low-histamine, easy foods. Keep safe, simple options stocked for flares: rice, fresh eggs, cooked fresh chicken, plain crackers, bone broth.

5

Contact your care team if the flare is prolonged or significantly worse than usual. A flare lasting more than 5–7 days, or a flare with new or different symptoms, warrants a call to your physician.

How to Communicate During a Flare

"I have hEDS and I'm in a flare. My baseline pain is [X]/10 and it's currently [X]/10. The flare started [X days ago]. My main symptoms are [joint pain / POTS / MCAS reactions]. I've tried rest, hydration, and antihistamines with limited improvement. I don't think this is an emergency but I need guidance on next steps."

Section 12: What to Avoid

Medications, activities, and substances that can worsen hEDS
This section is for general awareness only. Never stop a prescribed medication without discussing it with your doctor first. Some of these may be appropriate in certain situations despite the general cautions listed here.

Medications to Discuss Carefully with Your Doctor

Fluoroquinolone Antibiotics (Cipro, Levaquin, Avelox)

Fluoroquinolones carry an FDA Black Box Warning for tendon rupture and peripheral neuropathy. In patients with connective tissue disorders like hEDS, the risk is significantly elevated. These antibiotics inhibit collagen synthesis and can cause tendon damage — sometimes weeks after the course finishes. If a doctor prescribes a fluoroquinolone for a routine infection, ask whether an alternative (amoxicillin, doxycycline, azithromycin) can be used. In life-threatening infections where fluoroquinolones are the only option, benefit may outweigh risk — have that conversation with your physician.

NSAIDs in MCAS (Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Aspirin)

NSAIDs can trigger or worsen MCAS reactions by shifting arachidonic acid metabolism toward leukotrienes — potent mast cell activators. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is generally better tolerated for pain in MCAS patients. If you need anti-inflammatory effects and NSAIDs worsen your symptoms, ask your physician about COX-2 selective options.

Long-Term Opioids for Joint Pain

Opioids do not address the underlying instability driving hEDS pain and carry significant long-term risks including opioid-induced hyperalgesia (making pain worse over time) and hormonal disruption. Low dose naltrexone, nerve blocks, RFA, PRP, and stabilization PT address the mechanisms of hEDS pain more directly. If currently on opioids for hEDS, work with your pain physician on a comprehensive plan.

Long-Term Benzodiazepines for POTS-Related Anxiety

Many hEDS-POTS patients are prescribed benzodiazepines for "anxiety" when the underlying driver is sympathetic overdrive from dysautonomia. Benzos cause muscle relaxation that can worsen joint instability, and create dependence quickly. Treating the POTS addresses the anxiety more effectively.

Physical Approaches to Avoid

High-Velocity Cervical (Neck) Manipulation

Forceful "cracking" of the neck is contraindicated in hEDS due to cervical instability and the risk of vertebral artery injury. Always tell any manual therapist you have hEDS before any neck treatment. Low-force techniques are safe; high-velocity thrust is not.

Aggressive Stretching

You do not need more flexibility. Stretching hypermobile joints further damages already-lax ligaments and worsens instability. Yoga classes designed for flexibility are generally not appropriate. Restorative yoga is acceptable if you stay within a comfortable range — never push into end range.

High-Impact Exercise Before Stabilization

Running, jumping, HIIT, and heavy plyometrics load hypermobile joints without adequate stabilization. These are not permanently off limits — but they should only be introduced gradually after a period of stabilization work with a qualified PT.

Cervical Traction and Inversion Tables

These can be dangerous in hEDS due to cervical instability. Do not use without clearance from a physician who knows your imaging and understands hEDS.

Substances That Worsen POTS

  • Alcohol — causes vasodilation and dehydration, strongly worsens blood pooling. Even small amounts can trigger significant flares.
  • Caffeine (initially) — can worsen heart rate instability in acute POTS. Some patients tolerate small amounts once POTS is managed; others remain sensitive.
  • Heat exposure — hot baths, saunas, hot yoga, and hot weather all cause vasodilation that significantly worsens POTS. Cool or lukewarm showers are much better tolerated.
  • Large meals — postprandial blood pressure drop is common in POTS. Eat smaller, more frequent meals and avoid large carbohydrate loads.

Section 13: Doctor Appointment Toolkit

How to be heard, understood, and taken seriously in every appointment

The Single Most Important Thing to Understand

Physicians typically have 15–20 minutes per appointment. When a patient walks in with 12 symptoms across 6 body systems, a doctor's brain does not have time to form a coherent clinical picture. The appointment ends with a prescription for the loudest symptom and a follow-up in three months. The underlying condition is never addressed.

hEDS is uniquely difficult because it is a multi-system disorder — but that doesn't mean you have to present it that way. The most effective hEDS patients have learned to do one thing very well: organize their information so the doctor can see the pattern, not just the symptoms.

Your MyEDSTest clinical summary does this for you. It takes your validated screening results — Beighton score, pain severity, fatigue index, POTS screen, MCAS screen — and presents them in the clinical language physicians already understand. Hand it to the doctor at the start of the appointment. It immediately signals that you are a prepared, organized patient and frames the conversation before it begins.

Before Every Appointment — The 3-Item Preparation Rule

Walk into every appointment with exactly three things prepared. Not ten. Three.

1. Your MyEDSTest Summary Report

Print it or have it on your phone. It presents your Beighton score, BPI pain severity and interference scores, FSS fatigue score, POTS symptom count, and MCAS symptom count in one page. Physicians recognize these instruments. Instead of saying "I have a lot of pain," you can say "my BPI severity mean is 7.2 and interference mean is 6.8" — that's a clinical statement they can act on.

2. One Sentence That Frames the Visit

Write this out before you go. Example: "I'm here today to discuss my hypermobile EDS and get a referral to [specialist] because of [specific symptom]." One goal per visit. If you go in asking about your neck, your knee, your POTS, and your gut on the same day, you'll leave with nothing addressed properly.

3. The One Thing You Need from This Appointment

A referral. A specific test order. A prescription. A documentation of a finding. Know what you need before you walk in and ask for it directly at the start: "The main thing I need from today is [X]." This prevents the appointment from ending without your actual need being met.

Presenting Your Symptoms by System

Physicians are trained to think in body systems. When you organize your complaints by system rather than presenting them as a random list, you trigger the pattern-recognition part of a physician's clinical training. You become easier to understand and more credible.

Use this framework when summarizing your history to any new provider:

Musculoskeletal System

What to report: Joint hypermobility (Beighton score), subluxations/dislocations by joint, chronic pain locations and character, functional limitations.

"My Beighton score is [X]/9. I have chronic pain in [list regions] rated [X]/10 average severity. I have recurring subluxations in [joints]. My pain significantly interferes with [sleep/work/activity] — my BPI interference mean is [X]/10."

Autonomic / Cardiovascular System

What to report: POTS symptoms, heart rate on standing (if measured), fainting or near-fainting history, temperature dysregulation.

"I have [X]/8 POTS screening criteria. On home NASA lean testing my heart rate increased [X] bpm on standing. I experience [dizziness / brain fog / palpitations] when upright, relieved by lying down."

Immune / Mast Cell System

What to report: MCAS symptoms, food and chemical triggers, response to antihistamines, any prior allergy testing.

"I have [X]/7 MCAS screening criteria including [flushing / hives / gut reactions / chemical sensitivity]. I had [partial / significant] response to combined H1 and H2 antihistamines over a 6-week trial."

Neurological / Fatigue System

What to report: Brain fog severity and triggers, headache patterns, peripheral neuropathy symptoms, fatigue (FSS score), sleep quality.

"My FSS mean score is [X]/7, which is [above/below] the clinical significance threshold of 4.0. I experience [brain fog / headaches / numbness and tingling] that significantly impacts [daily function / work / driving]."

Gastrointestinal System

What to report: IBS-type symptoms, gut motility problems, nausea, food intolerances, reflux.

"I have significant gastrointestinal symptoms including [list]. These are consistent with the gut connective tissue involvement and MCAS component of hEDS. I'd like to understand whether a gastroenterology referral is appropriate."

Practice this before your appointment. Say it out loud at home. It feels unnatural at first — but organized, concise patients get dramatically better care. You are not being difficult; you are making the physician's job easier.

Scripts by Specialist

Primary Care — Requesting Referrals

"I've done a structured self-assessment using the 2017 international hEDS diagnostic criteria and validated instruments. My Beighton Score is [X]/9, my BPI pain severity mean is [X]/10, my FSS fatigue score is [X]/7, and I have [X]/8 POTS screening criteria. I'd like referrals to a rheumatologist or geneticist for formal hEDS evaluation, and a cardiologist for POTS workup. I've prepared a one-page clinical summary if that would be helpful."

Cardiologist — POTS Evaluation

"I have suspected or confirmed hEDS — POTS is present in approximately 40% of hEDS patients. I have [X]/8 POTS symptoms on a validated screening tool, including [dizziness on standing / racing heart / brain fog]. My home NASA lean test showed a heart rate increase of [X] bpm on standing. I'm requesting a formal tilt-table test to evaluate for POTS and guidance on treatment options."

Interventional Pain Physician — First Visit

"I have hypermobile EDS with chronic pain primarily in [neck / back / joints — be specific]. My BPI pain severity average is [X]/10 and interference average is [X]/10. I'm not seeking opioid medications. I'm here to understand my options for injection-based treatments — specifically [SI joint injections / facet blocks / occipital nerve blocks / RFA]. I'd also like to discuss low dose naltrexone and whether I'm a candidate for orthobiologic treatments like PRP."

Allergist / Immunologist — MCAS Evaluation

"I have [X]/7 MCAS screening criteria, including [flushing / reactions to foods or chemicals / gut symptoms / urticaria]. Over a 6-week trial of combined levocetirizine 5mg and famotidine 20mg twice daily, I had [partial / significant] symptom improvement. I'd like a formal MCAS evaluation including serum tryptase baseline and 24-hour urine N-methylhistamine."

Rheumatologist — hEDS Confirmation

"I'm being evaluated for hypermobile EDS using the 2017 international diagnostic criteria. I have a Beighton score of [X]/9, [X] connective tissue systemic features on Criterion 2, and I've been told I meet Criterion 3 with a family history of hypermobility. I'm not expecting a genetic test — I understand hEDS doesn't have one yet. I'd like a clinical evaluation to confirm or refine the diagnosis and discuss management."

Any Specialist — Requesting a Specific Treatment or Test

"I've researched [treatment/test] in the context of hEDS and I believe it may be relevant to my presentation. I understand it may not be your standard protocol, but I'd value your clinical opinion on whether it's appropriate for my specific case. If you feel it's not indicated, can you help me understand the reasoning so I can make the most informed decision possible?"

Managing a 15-Minute Appointment Effectively

Start

Hand over your MyEDSTest summary immediately. Before the doctor asks "so what brings you in today" — hand them the summary and say: "I've prepared a clinical summary to help organize my history. The main thing I need today is [X]." You've just used the first 30 seconds to set the agenda.

Middle

Stick to one system per visit whenever possible. If you're at a pain visit, stay on pain. If you're at a cardiology visit, stay on POTS. Trying to address every system in a 15-minute visit guarantees nothing is fully addressed. Book separate follow-ups for separate systems.

End

Before the doctor leaves, confirm the plan. "Before we finish — can I confirm what we decided today? You're ordering [test], sending a referral to [specialist], and I should follow up in [timeframe]. Is that correct?" This prevents the common situation where the visit felt productive but nothing actually got ordered.

After

Check your patient portal within 48 hours. Confirm that referrals were actually sent, lab orders are in the system, and notes accurately reflect what was discussed. Errors in medical records happen frequently — you are your own best advocate for catching them.

How to Handle Dismissal

If a doctor dismisses your symptoms, tells you it's anxiety, or says "you look fine" — you have options. Your response depends on how much you want to preserve the relationship with this provider.

Option 1 — Engage and redirect (preserves relationship)

"I appreciate that anxiety can cause some of these symptoms. I want to make sure we've ruled out a connective tissue disorder first — the pattern of my symptoms across multiple systems is consistent with hEDS. Can we document in my chart that hEDS was considered and your clinical reasoning for ruling it out? I want to be a good partner in my own care."

Asking for documentation creates accountability. Physicians are less likely to dismiss documented concerns without a clear rationale.

Option 2 — Request a referral anyway

"I understand you may not think this is EDS. I respect your clinical judgment. I'd still like a referral to rheumatology for a second opinion — I want to hear a specialist's perspective before we close the door on this. Is that something you can arrange?"

Option 3 — Find a new provider

Seeking a second opinion or finding a new provider is not "doctor shopping" — it is your right as a patient. Providers who understand complex connective tissue disorders exist. Use the EDS Society healthcare directory at ehlers-danlos.com/healthcare-professionals-directory. Telehealth has made it possible to access EDS-knowledgeable physicians across the country without leaving your home.

Building Your Long-Term Care Team

hEDS requires a team, not a single physician. The goal is to build a group of providers who each own one piece of your care and communicate with each other. This takes time to assemble — but once in place, it transforms your experience with the medical system.

Primary Care Physician

Coordinates your care, orders labs, manages referrals, and serves as the central hub. Find one who understands or is willing to learn about hEDS.

Interventional Pain Physician

Manages pain with injections, nerve blocks, RFA, and orthobiologics. The most impactful specialist for day-to-day hEDS pain management.

Cardiologist or Autonomic Specialist

Evaluates and manages POTS. Ask specifically for someone who sees dysautonomia patients — not all cardiologists are familiar with POTS in hEDS.

Hypermobility-Trained PT

Manages joint stabilization, strength, and pacing. The most important ongoing provider for functional improvement. See Section 5 for how to find one.

Allergist / Immunologist

Evaluates and manages MCAS. Not all allergists see MCAS — ask specifically when booking whether they have experience with mast cell activation.

Rheumatologist or Geneticist

Provides formal diagnosis and rules out other connective tissue disorders (Marfan syndrome, vEDS). Important for documentation, especially for disability or insurance purposes.

Start here

You don't need all of these at once. Start with a primary care physician willing to coordinate, one referral to the specialist most relevant to your dominant symptoms, and one hypermobility-aware PT. Build from there.
